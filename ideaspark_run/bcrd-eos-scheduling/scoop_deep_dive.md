# Scoop Check Step 5: full-paper deep dive

## Proposed-work reference frame

The comparison target is **Budgeted Counterfactual Regret Distillation (BCRD)** as stated in the research intake:

- **Problem framing:** learn a fast scheduler for large, state-dependent feasible sets of task–satellite–observation-window actions from solver schedules that reveal the chosen action but not the opportunity cost of feasible rejected actions.
- **Core mechanism:** warm-start from heterogeneous solver trajectories; at selected offline states, force a budgeted informative subset of feasible alternatives; run deterministic cheap finite-horizon continuations; convert returns to local normalized regret; train a shared policy head and nonnegative regret head; and decode over the full feasible set with policy score minus beta times predicted regret.
- **Key insight:** one-hot behavior cloning is mis-specified because feasible unselected actions have heterogeneous downstream opportunity costs. Sparse training-time counterfactual evaluation can expose that structure without retaining rollout/search cost at inference.
- **Application domain:** offline, deterministic EOS-Bench scheduling, potentially spanning agile/non-agile instances and large, variable candidate sets. Learner-state aggregation is optional rather than the primary contribution.

In the records below, **match** means that the paper contains the same substantive axis, **partial** means that it contains a close abstraction but differs in an important technical condition, and **differ** means that it does not contain that axis. Line references are to the supplied extracted full texts.

## 1. Ross and Bagnell (2014), “Reinforcement and Imitation Learning via Interactive No-Regret Learning” (AggreVaTe)

- **Venue (verified):** CoRR/arXiv preprint, arXiv:1406.5979 (2014). The supplied paper does not identify a peer-reviewed venue.
- **Problem framing (verified):** finite-horizon imitation learning in an MDP with bounded per-step cost. A learned policy changes the state distribution on which it is evaluated, and plain action-agreement training treats all expert disagreements alike. The objective is a policy with low total task cost, using an expert that can supply or induce state–action cost-to-go values at states reached by the learner.
- **Core mechanism (verified):** on each interactive trajectory, roll in with the current learner (optionally mixed with the expert), choose a random time, force an exploration action, then hand control to the expert through the remaining horizon. Store the resulting state, time, action, and expert cost-to-go; aggregate the data across iterations; and train an online cost-sensitive classifier. With full feedback, the target is a cost-to-go vector over all actions; with partial feedback, the paper permits one explored action and proposes regression or bandit reductions. At deployment, the learned classifier/regressor acts without the expert.
- **Key insight (verified):** the severity of an imitation error is its downstream cost, not merely whether it disagrees with the demonstrator. Minimizing expert cost-to-go yields a statistical-regret reduction and can teach the learner to avoid unrecoverable states that ordinary imitation labels cannot distinguish.
- **Domain and scale:** a general theoretical treatment of finite-horizon control, imitation learning, structured prediction, and approximate policy iteration. Driving, robotics, game AI, and structured-output examples are motivational; this paper reports no empirical dataset or satellite-scheduling experiment.
- **Assumptions and scope:** the expert is assumed to be good and its cost-to-go is known or estimable by rollout; the learner can interactively visit states and query a deviation; costs and cost-to-go are bounded; and the policy class should contain behavior sufficiently close to the expert. The authors explicitly warn that expert cost-to-go can be over-optimistic when the expert is substantially more capable than the learner. A single state–action label may require a full remaining trajectory, making the method much more expensive than DAgger-style action labels.
- **Three concrete evidence pointers:**
  1. **Section 2.1, lines 75–89:** defines the bounded finite-horizon MDP, cost-to-go, learner-induced state distribution, and the non-i.i.d. distribution-shift problem.
  2. **Section 2.2 and Algorithm 1, lines 93–149:** rolls in with the learner, forces one action, lets the expert complete the trajectory, aggregates cost-to-go examples, and trains a cost-sensitive classifier.
  3. **Section 4 limitations, lines 470–478:** explains that each cost-to-go label may consume an entire trajectory and suggests first learning a reasonable imitation policy before spending expensive refinement queries.
- **Refined four-axis overlap with BCRD:**
  - **Problem framing — partial:** both replace action agreement with downstream-sensitive supervision in sequential decision making, but AggreVaTe assumes interactive learner-state access and a queryable expert rather than fixed offline solver traces with large dynamic feasible sets.
  - **Core mechanism — partial, strong:** forced alternatives, continuation-derived state–action costs, aggregation, and a no-oracle inference policy are direct precedents. AggreVaTe does not provide BCRD’s informative budget allocator, cheap truncated deterministic continuation design, local normalization, separate nonnegative regret head, or policy-minus-regret decoder.
  - **Key insight — match:** both reject the premise that every non-expert action should receive the same penalty and use downstream consequences to distinguish errors.
  - **Application domain — differ:** generic imitation/control theory rather than Earth-observation scheduling.
- **Novelty threat:** **high conceptual threat, not a full scoop.** BCRD cannot claim that counterfactual cost-to-go labels improve over one-hot imitation in general; AggreVaTe already establishes that idea and the training-time-query/deployment-time-policy pattern. The defensible delta is the offline, budgeted, truncated, EOS-specific construction and the explicit regret-head decoder, not “cost-aware imitation” itself.

## 2. Leblond et al. (2018), “SeaRNN: Training RNNs with Global-Local Losses”

- **Venue (verified):** International Conference on Learning Representations (ICLR), 2018.
- **Problem framing (verified):** train a recurrent structured predictor whose local next-token choices are normally supervised by maximum likelihood. MLE both exposes the model only to ground-truth histories and assigns no graded distinction between locally plausible and disastrous alternatives under the sequence-level task metric. The output is a sequence; training has paired ground-truth input/output sequences and a task loss.
- **Core mechanism (verified):** generate a roll-in trajectory; at each selected cell, force a candidate token and complete the sequence with a reference, learned, or mixed rollout policy; score the completed sequence with the actual task loss; and form a local cost vector carrying global sequence information. The RNN is updated with either a log loss aimed at the minimum-cost token or a KL/cross-entropy loss against a softmax distribution derived from the costs. To control the original |A|T rollout burden, SeaRNN subsamples cells and tokens using uniform, policy, low-probability-biased, or top-k selection, always including the ground-truth token; the softmax is restricted to sampled tokens. Rollouts are a training procedure, while ordinary greedy/beam RNN decoding is used at test time.
- **Key insight (verified):** a local learning signal can contain the downstream effect of a decision if it is derived from the completed structured output. This global-local target distinguishes good and bad alternatives, addresses exposure bias through learned roll-in, and can optimize non-differentiable task losses because rollout costs are held fixed during each gradient step.
- **Domain and scale:** OCR (26 actions, maximum length 15), spelling correction, and IWSLT 2014 German-to-English translation. The translation experiment uses about 153,000/7,000/7,000 train/validation/test sentence pairs and vocabularies of 22,822 English and 32,009 German words; 25 tokens per cell are sampled for SeaRNN.
- **Assumptions and scope:** requires supervised paired outputs and a computable terminal or structured loss. It must choose a usable roll-in/rollout policy; the nominal full method costs |A|T forward rollouts per example. Under subsampling, unsampled alternatives receive no direct first-order update and the useful target must be present among sampled tokens. The NMT experiment needed a reference roll-in because learned roll-in made optimization unreliable. Costs are fixed rather than differentiated through.
- **Three concrete evidence pointers:**
  1. **Section 4 and Algorithm 1, lines 247–290:** forces every candidate at a local cell, completes the sequence, converts terminal task error to a cost vector, and takes a gradient step on the resulting local losses.
  2. **Section 5, lines 447–504:** identifies the |A|T rollout bottleneck, introduces cell/token subsampling and top-k or policy-guided selection, and restricts the loss to sampled candidates.
  3. **Section 6, lines 543–565:** gives the IWSLT scale, 25-token sampling choice, deterministic cost computation using smoothed sentence BLEU, and evaluation setup.
- **Refined four-axis overlap with BCRD:**
  - **Problem framing — partial, strong:** both diagnose a one-hot local target that fails to rank feasible alternatives by downstream structured loss. SeaRNN uses token sequences with a fixed vocabulary and ground-truth output, not task–satellite–window scheduling actions learned from solver traces.
  - **Core mechanism — partial, very strong:** forced alternatives, deterministic completion, local cost vectors, strategic subsampling, and no rollout at deployment closely match BCRD’s central training pattern. SeaRNN lacks an offline solver-trajectory interface, EOS feasibility machinery, finite-horizon cheap continuation as a deliberate approximation, explicit normalized regret targets, a nonnegative regret head, and the policy-minus-regret decoder.
  - **Key insight — match:** both distribute full-output consequences back to individual local decisions so alternatives are not treated as uniformly wrong.
  - **Application domain — differ:** language/vision structured prediction rather than combinatorial satellite scheduling.
- **Novelty threat:** **very high and the closest single-paper method threat.** “Budgeted counterfactual rollout supervision with no test-time rollout” is already present in substance. BCRD must be framed as a scheduling-specific offline distillation method with a new candidate-budget policy, continuation approximation, regret calibration/head, and decoding rule; presenting generic counterfactual subsampling as the novelty would be indefensible.

## 3. Herrmann and Schaub (2022), “Monte Carlo Tree Search Methods for the Earth-Observing Satellite Scheduling Problem”

- **Venue (verified):** *Journal of Aerospace Information Systems*, volume 19, issue 1, January 2022; DOI 10.2514/1.I010992.
- **Problem framing (verified):** closed-loop onboard scheduling for one Earth-observing spacecraft and seven ground stations over a 270-minute horizon divided into 6-minute decision intervals. The scheduler chooses among four flight modes—image, downlink, charge, desaturate—to maximize returned science data while preventing battery depletion, data-buffer overflow, and reaction-wheel saturation. It is target-agnostic nadir imaging, not request assignment to satellite/window candidates.
- **Core mechanism (verified):** use UCT-style MCTS with random or safety-aware heuristic rollout to estimate Q(s,a) in a high-fidelity Basilisk generative simulator. For main-tree states actually used to advance the solution, retain the state and all four state–action values, update them with realized return, and train neural Q approximators on a 90/10 split. At inference, input the current state, score the four modes, and take argmax Q without MCTS. The best networks contain roughly 200,000–1.3 million parameters.
- **Key insight (verified):** costly online search can serve as a data generator whose action values are compressed into a neural network, preserving near-optimal closed-loop behavior at flight-computer-compatible inference speed. Safety-aware rollouts make the search data substantially better than random rollouts.
- **Domain and scale:** exact Earth-observation operations domain, but one spacecraft, seven stations, four fixed actions, approximately three orbits/45 decisions, and 100 held-out initial conditions. The value network averages 0.0672 seconds versus 11,400 seconds for heuristic MCTS, with reported 1.3% versus 0.64% optimality gaps relative to the genetic-algorithm reference.
- **Assumptions and scope:** deterministic finite-horizon simulator with a truth generative model available to MCTS; fixed four-action abstraction; no specific imaging targets or task/window allocation; nadir pointing; fixed semimajor axis and training epoch distributions; and a 270-minute horizon. The paper documents failures under epoch and large altitude shifts, showing sensitivity to omitted state variables and training support. Values away from the main tree are discarded because they are poorly visited.
- **Three concrete evidence pointers:**
  1. **Section II, lines 203–240:** specifies the single-satellite objective, resource constraints, deterministic MDP, four flight-mode actions, 6-minute intervals, and exclusion of target requests.
  2. **Section IV.B, lines 391–413:** describes MCTS expansion over all actions, terminal/safety rollouts, value backup, and selecting the maximum-Q action to advance the real environment.
  3. **Section IV.C, lines 477–519:** turns each main-tree state and its four Q values into regression data, trains Q networks, and defines deployment as argmax over predicted state–action values.
- **Refined four-axis overlap with BCRD:**
  - **Problem framing — partial:** both seek fast learned EOS decisions informed by downstream values, but this paper schedules resource-management modes with four fixed actions rather than large dynamic task–satellite–window candidate sets learned from selected solver actions.
  - **Core mechanism — partial, strong:** MCTS value generation followed by neural action-value distillation and search-free argmax inference is a direct same-domain precedent. It does not start from heterogeneous solver traces, allocate sparse counterfactual queries, form regret relative to the demonstrated action, use cheap truncated continuations, or train a separate regret-adjusted policy.
  - **Key insight — partial:** both move expensive planning computation to training and retain a fast scorer at deployment. Herrmann–Schaub’s rationale is compression of search Q values and safety, not correcting the false negatives created by one-hot behavior cloning.
  - **Application domain — partial, strong:** the same broad EOS scheduling domain, but a materially different target-agnostic single-spacecraft mode-scheduling variant and far smaller action space.
- **Novelty threat:** **high as the strongest domain-specific distillation precedent, but not a method scoop.** BCRD cannot claim to be the first to distill search-derived state–action consequences into a search-free EOS policy. Its remaining novelty lies in doing so from sparse offline schedule traces over huge dynamic candidate sets, under a counterfactual budget, with local regret calibration and a two-head decoder.

## 4. Jacquet et al. (2025), “Improved Earth Observation Satellite Scheduling with Graph Neural Networks and Monte Carlo Tree Search”

- **Venue (verified):** arXiv preprint, arXiv:2408.15041v2 (26 November 2025). No peer-reviewed venue is disclosed in the supplied version.
- **Problem framing (verified):** select and order an oversubscribed set of candidate acquisitions for a single agile satellite over one orbit, respecting continuous visibility windows and time-dependent maneuver delays, to maximize cumulative utility. The paper explicitly excludes multi-satellite, multi-orbit, multi-shot, and stochastic cloud-cover cases.
- **Core mechanism (verified):** derive a compact continuous graph with one node per acquisition from a much larger time-discretized feasibility graph. A rewired message-passing GNN, global pooling node, actor, and critic are trained with masked PPO; the GNN emits one logit per candidate acquisition and remains size-agnostic. At inference, optional PUCT MCTS refines the learned policy, seeds expanded nodes with the learned value function, and copies the parent value to unexplored children to cope with large branching factors and small budgets.
- **Key insight (verified):** graph rewiring and global pooling let the actor represent future conflicts while remaining agnostic to instance size, supporting training on small instances and transfer to larger ones. Because the learned greedy policy remains imperfect, explicit search at resolution time can improve it.
- **Domain and scale:** private real-life single-satellite/single-orbit instances with 100–1,591 candidate acquisitions, usually only about 50 selected. Training experiments use 128 or 639 problems near 100 acquisitions; evaluation includes individual larger instances. Inference MCTS budgets of 100 and 1,000 improve utility but take roughly 138 and 1,248 minutes on the reported test set.
- **Assumptions and scope:** deterministic environment; earliest-possible discretized start time after selecting an acquisition; transition information supplied by a precomputed graph from a closed-source proprietary simulator; partial observability of exact transition durations; chronological insertion only; private datasets; and no uncertainty. The paper notes degraded generalization on the largest instance and that inference search is far from negligible.
- **Three concrete evidence pointers:**
  1. **Introduction and Section 3, lines 17–97:** defines the oversubscribed single-satellite, single-orbit, single-shot utility-maximization task and its visibility/maneuver constraints.
  2. **Section 4, lines 249–324:** describes the continuous-graph PPO/GNN actor–critic, feasible-action masking, reverse edges/global context, and per-node action logits.
  3. **Sections 5.3–6, lines 489–577:** reports inference-time MCTS budgets, learned-value initialization, long solve times, shallow coverage of the search tree, and the chronological-insertion limitation.
- **Refined four-axis overlap with BCRD:**
  - **Problem framing — partial, strong:** both sequentially choose from changing feasible observation candidates and care about downstream conflicts; Jacquet et al. omit the satellite dimension and use private single-orbit instances.
  - **Core mechanism — differ:** masked PPO plus GNN representation and inference-time MCTS do not create offline counterfactual regret labels or a regret head. Its search occurs at deployment, the opposite of BCRD’s no-rollout inference constraint.
  - **Key insight — partial:** both need to anticipate how a local acquisition affects later opportunities, but Jacquet et al. encode that through message passing and resolution-time search rather than graded supervision for rejected feasible actions.
  - **Application domain — match at the broad level / partial at scope:** agile EOS acquisition scheduling, but only a private single-satellite/single-orbit variant.
- **Novelty threat:** **medium.** This is a mandatory same-domain neural baseline and blocks broad claims about learned policies for large changing acquisition sets or GNN generalization. It does not threaten the counterfactual-regret training contribution, and its expensive inference search sharpens BCRD’s intended deployment-time advantage.

## 5. Yin et al. (2026), “EOS-Bench: A Comprehensive Benchmark for Earth Observation Satellite Scheduling”

- **Venue (verified):** arXiv preprint, arXiv:2604.25782v1 (28 April 2026). The supplied version does not identify a peer-reviewed venue.
- **Problem framing (verified):** create a unified, solver-agnostic benchmark for selecting, allocating, and sequencing Earth-observation requests under access windows, maneuver compatibility, task uniqueness, energy, and memory constraints. A candidate is a satellite–task–window–start-time assignment; a solver returns a feasible subset/schedule and is evaluated on total profit, completion rate, workload balance, timeliness, and runtime.
- **Core mechanism (verified):** generate a library of 1,390 scenarios/13,900 instances across platform type, horizon, constellation size, task load, target distribution, and resource settings; compute pre-optimization descriptors for opportunity sparsity, flexibility, pairwise conflict, and timeline congestion; and expose one common interface to 17 exact, greedy, metaheuristic, and PPO baselines. The PPO baseline sequentially selects a masked task–satellite–window action or skip, with task profit as reward.
- **Key insight (verified):** nominal instance size alone does not characterize EOS scheduling difficulty. Shared physically motivated scenario families, structural descriptors, and a common multi-metric protocol are needed to explain solver behavior and make results comparable.
- **Domain and scale:** agile and non-agile optical point-target scheduling from 1–1,000 satellites, 10–10,000 tasks, and 1–168-hour horizons. The library has 7,130 agile and 6,770 non-agile instances; the paper’s detailed experiments use 250 scenarios/2,500 instances and include MIP, greedy, GA, SA, ACO, and PPO families.
- **Assumptions and scope:** offline and deterministic with all tasks and windows known; point targets; simplified attitude-transition and normalized resource models; optical payload only; no cloud, task failure, onboard replanning, detailed hardware calibration, or integrated downlink. The benchmark paper explicitly states that it proposes an evaluation platform rather than a new scheduling algorithm.
- **Three concrete evidence pointers:**
  1. **Section 3.5, lines 1032–1070:** formalizes task–satellite–window–time candidate assignments, compatibility/resource constraints, feasible schedules, and the solver-agnostic feasible-set view.
  2. **Section 3.6, lines 1083–1126:** bounds the current benchmark to deterministic offline point-target scheduling with simplified resource and attitude models.
  3. **Section 7.1.4, lines 1931–1951:** defines the masked PPO action as a task–satellite–access-window assignment or skip and gives the full 13,900-instance scale.
- **Refined four-axis overlap with BCRD:**
  - **Problem framing — match for the benchmark task, differ for the learning gap:** BCRD operates on exactly this feasible scheduling space, but EOS-Bench does not formulate the selected-only-label problem.
  - **Core mechanism — differ:** EOS-Bench supplies data generation, metrics, and baselines; it contains no solver-trace imitation, forced counterfactual continuation, regret targets, or regret-adjusted decoder.
  - **Key insight — differ:** its contribution is benchmark comparability and structural characterization, not correcting one-hot behavior-cloning labels.
  - **Application domain — match:** this is the direct benchmark and evaluation domain for BCRD.
- **Novelty threat:** **low as a method threat, decisive as the problem/evaluation anchor.** The paper does not scoop BCRD, but it prevents claiming the benchmark, action formalization, large-scale scenario range, solver pool, or masked PPO baseline as new. BCRD must demonstrate added value over the benchmark’s existing solver classes under its five metrics and declared deterministic/offline scope.

## 6. Chang et al. (2015), “Learning to Search Better than Your Teacher” (LOLS)

- **Venue (verified):** 32nd International Conference on Machine Learning (ICML), 2015; PMLR 37.
- **Problem framing (verified):** structured prediction as a deterministic fixed-depth search problem with state-dependent legal actions and a terminal structured loss. A reference policy may be suboptimal or outside the learner’s hypothesis class, so merely matching it is insufficient. The goal is a learned policy that is competitive with the reference while also robust to its own one-step action deviations.
- **Core mechanism (verified):** roll in with the current learned policy to each decision point; for every legal action, force that one-step deviation and roll out to the end with either the reference or current learner, selected as a mixture; compute a local cost c(a) equal to the terminal loss after action a minus the minimum terminal loss among actions; and update an online no-regret cost-sensitive multiclass learner. Thus the target is explicitly a local regret-to-best action. The main full-information algorithm evaluates all legal actions; a separate structured contextual-bandit variant handles one observed loss with randomized exploration.
- **Key insight (verified):** reference roll-in hides learner-induced states, while reference-only rollout can conceal compounding errors when the teacher is suboptimal. Learned roll-in plus mixed rollout produces supervision at realistic states and trades off performance relative to the teacher against local improvement over the learner’s own policy.
- **Domain and scale:** cost-sensitive multiclass classification on KDDCup 99 (4,898,431 train and 311,029 test examples), Penn Treebank POS tagging (38,000 train and 11,000 test sentences), and transition-based dependency parsing on standard Penn Treebank splits. These have small per-state action sets and labeled structured outputs, not satellite assignment sets.
- **Assumptions and scope:** deterministic transitions, a fixed horizon, end-state loss, state features, a reference policy derived from labels, and access to an online no-regret cost-sensitive classifier. The main algorithm requires a terminal rollout for every legal action at every decision. Its local-optimality guarantee may require exponentially many policy moves without additional structure, and the quality/realizability of the reference and policy class affects the guarantee.
- **Three concrete evidence pointers:**
  1. **Algorithm 1 and Section 2, lines 169–228:** uses learned roll-in, enumerates every legal action, performs mixed reference/learner rollouts, and trains on the resulting cost-sensitive examples.
  2. **Equation 2, lines 207–225:** defines each local action cost as rollout loss minus the best rollout loss, the closest explicit precursor to BCRD’s local regret label.
  3. **Section 5, lines 515–587:** gives the KDD/Penn Treebank tasks and scales and empirically shows that learned roll-in plus mixed rollout helps when the reference is weak.
- **Refined four-axis overlap with BCRD:**
  - **Problem framing — partial, strong:** both learn sequential decisions from a teacher/reference and examine legal rejected actions because their downstream losses differ. LOLS assumes labeled structured examples and is explicitly interactive/online rather than solver-only offline trajectories.
  - **Core mechanism — partial, very strong:** forced legal alternatives, rollout completion, and loss-minus-best local regret are direct precedents. LOLS evaluates all actions to terminal depth, uses a cost-sensitive classifier, and has no informative budget, truncated cheap continuation, normalization scheme beyond subtracting the minimum, separate regret head, or policy-minus-regret inference.
  - **Key insight — partial, strong:** both use one-step deviations to discover locally better actions. LOLS emphasizes beating a suboptimal reference and learner-state distribution; BCRD emphasizes false-negative labels among feasible actions and makes learner aggregation secondary.
  - **Application domain — differ:** NLP and generic structured search, not EOS scheduling.
- **Novelty threat:** **very high for the regret-label construction and the second-closest method threat.** BCRD cannot claim novelty for forcing alternatives, rolling them out, or defining local regret relative to the best continuation. The defensible contribution is a budgeted offline approximation suitable for huge EOS feasible sets plus the two-head calibrated decoder and an empirical demonstration that it works with heterogeneous solver traces.

## 7. Sun et al. (2017), “Deeply AggreVaTeD: Differentiable Imitation Learning for Sequential Prediction”

- **Venue (verified):** 34th International Conference on Machine Learning (ICML), Sydney, 2017; PMLR 70.
- **Problem framing (verified):** learn expressive differentiable policies for finite-horizon sequential prediction/control using a training-time expert or near-optimal cost-to-go oracle that is unavailable at test time. The learner should approach or exceed the expert while avoiding the sample inefficiency and delayed-reward problem of plain reinforcement learning.
- **Core mechanism (verified):** roll out a mixture of expert and current learner to collect trajectories under the learner-relevant state distribution; weight the policy’s action gradients by expert Q-values or advantages; and update deep, recurrent, discrete-action, or continuous-action policies with ordinary or natural policy gradient. In discrete settings the exact gradient sums over actions; practical estimators use finite sampled trajectories. The method does not need to retain all past data and uses the learned policy alone at inference.
- **Key insight (verified):** a strong cost-to-go oracle supplies dense long-horizon credit assignment that plain RL must discover through exploration. Differentiating the AggreVaTe loss allows this oracle information to train neural/RNN and continuous-action policies efficiently, with variance reduction via expert advantage baselines.
- **Domain and scale:** OpenAI Gym CartPole (horizon 500), Acrobot (horizon 200), MuJoCo Hopper and Walker (horizon 100, 50 trajectories/iteration), partially observed control with LSTMs/GRUs, and handwritten-algebra dependency parsing on 400 equation sets. The parsing split is 80/10/10 and uses recurrent policies over a small transition action set.
- **Assumptions and scope:** finite horizon; an expert Q function known or estimable without bias for queried state–action pairs; interactive trajectories from a simulator/environment; a stochastic differentiable policy; and an expert that is near-optimal for the strongest theory. Nonlinear neural policies lose the convex AggreVaTe guarantee and obtain only local convergence intuition. Continuous action uses importance-weighted sampled actions rather than enumerating alternatives.
- **Three concrete evidence pointers:**
  1. **Sections 2–3, lines 180–221:** defines expert Q, assumes it is known or unbiasedly estimable by force-action-then-expert rollout, and minimizes that cost under the learner’s state distribution.
  2. **Sections 3.1–4 and Algorithm 1, lines 256–327:** derives Q-weighted policy gradients, mixes expert and learner roll-in, estimates gradients from trajectories, and returns a policy selected on validation.
  3. **Section 6, lines 423–483:** specifies the robotics tasks, oracle construction, horizons, trajectory counts, network sizes, and partial-observation experiments.
- **Refined four-axis overlap with BCRD:**
  - **Problem framing — partial:** both use training-time downstream values to train a fast oracle-free sequential policy, but Deeply AggreVaTeD is interactive and assumes queryable expert Q rather than selected-only offline schedules.
  - **Core mechanism — partial:** expert-Q/advantage-weighted neural updates are related to regret-sensitive learning, but the paper neither selects a budgeted counterfactual action set nor creates explicit local regret labels or a separate regret head.
  - **Key insight — partial, strong:** both exploit cheap-at-inference distillation of long-horizon consequences, although Deeply AggreVaTeD’s central comparison is imitation versus RL sample efficiency rather than one-hot false negatives.
  - **Application domain — differ:** robotics and parsing, not EOS scheduling.
- **Novelty threat:** **medium-high conceptual threat, not a direct scoop.** It blocks claims that BCRD is the first neural, differentiable, training-time-Q imitation method or the first whose oracle disappears at inference. Its gradient estimator, oracle assumptions, and interactive data regime remain materially different from BCRD’s offline budgeted label-generation and two-head decoding.

## Cross-paper novelty assessment

### Concise overlap matrix

| Prior work | Problem framing | Core mechanism | Key insight | Application domain | Threat summary |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| AggreVaTe (2014) | Partial | Partial—strong | Match | Differ | High conceptual precedent for cost-to-go rather than one-hot imitation |
| SeaRNN (2018) | Partial—strong | Partial—very strong | Match | Differ | Closest general-method threat: subsampled forced alternatives, deterministic completion, local costs, no rollout at test |
| Herrmann–Schaub (2022) | Partial | Partial—strong | Partial | Partial—strong | **Closest same-domain prior:** MCTS Q targets distilled into a search-free EOS value policy |
| Jacquet et al. (2025) | Partial—strong | Differ | Partial | Match/partial in scope | Same-domain neural/search baseline, but search remains at inference and no regret labels are learned |
| EOS-Bench (2026) | Match for task | Differ | Differ | Match | Evaluation anchor rather than a BCRD-like scheduling method |
| LOLS (2015) | Partial—strong | Partial—very strong | Partial—strong | Differ | Closest explicit local-regret target: rollout loss minus best rollout loss |
| Deeply AggreVaTeD (2017) | Partial | Partial | Partial—strong | Differ | Neural training-time-Q precedent, but interactive and gradient-based |

The deepest issue is **compositional prior art**:

1. **SeaRNN is the closest single-paper method threat:** it already combines forced local alternatives, deterministic completion to structured loss, strategic candidate subsampling, graded local targets, and ordinary no-rollout inference.
2. **LOLS is the closest explicit regret-target threat:** its Equation 2 is already rollout loss minus the best alternative’s rollout loss, with learned-state roll-in and one-step deviations.
3. **Herrmann–Schaub is the closest domain-specific distillation threat:** it already uses MCTS state–action values as neural training targets and deploys an argmax value policy without search in an EOS problem.

No supplied paper contains the full BCRD combination: heterogeneous offline solver traces that expose only selected actions; an explicitly informative counterfactual budget for huge dynamic task–satellite–window sets; deterministic cheap *finite-horizon* continuation rather than full terminal or heavy MCTS evaluation; local normalization plus a constrained nonnegative regret head; and full-set decoding that combines a policy score with predicted regret. The proposal is therefore **not fully scooped by any one candidate**, but its broad conceptual ingredients are crowded. A defensible method claim must make that exact integration—and its measured compute/quality trade-off—the contribution.

The most reviewer-resistant delta is:

> Unlike SeaRNN and LOLS, which obtain local cost/regret supervision by rolling structured predictions over token-scale action sets with labeled references, BCRD reconstructs sparse counterfactual regret from selected-only heterogeneous EOS solver traces under an explicit labeling budget, learns a separate nonnegative regret correction, and retains full-set scheduler inference without rollout.
